## Furnace Installation Considerations

**H2:** What to Consider Before Installing a New Furnace

**Section intro (1 sentence):**
The right furnace for a 1,400 sq ft Menifee ranch home is a different system than the right furnace for a 2,800 sq ft two-story in Rancho Cucamonga — here's what drives the decision.

---

**Card 1**
**Title:** AFUE Rating — What It Means for Your Energy Bills
**Description:** AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency) measures how much of the fuel your furnace burns actually becomes heat in your home. An 80% AFUE furnace wastes 20 cents of every fuel dollar out the flue. A 95% AFUE system wastes only 5 cents. According to ENERGY STAR, upgrading from an older 80% AFUE unit to a 90%+ system typically saves 10–20% on annual heating costs — meaningful over a 15–20 year furnace lifespan even in the Inland Empire's shorter heating season. Standard installations use 90%+ AFUE two-stage systems; we also install 95%+ variable-speed units for maximum efficiency.

---

**Card 2**
**Title:** Proper Sizing — Why BTUs Matter More Than You Think
**Description:** An oversized furnace heats too fast, shuts off, and cycles on and off repeatedly — stressing components and leaving uneven temperatures through the house. An undersized furnace runs constantly and never reaches setpoint during cold nights. Proper sizing requires a Manual J load calculation that factors your home's square footage, insulation level, window count, ceiling height, and local climate data. We perform this calculation on the in-home estimate — we don't size by square footage rule of thumb.

---

**Card 3**
**Title:** Gas vs. Electric — Choosing the Right Fuel Source
**Description:** Most Inland Empire homes are served by SoCalGas and use gas furnaces. Gas heat is typically faster and less expensive per BTU than electric resistance heating. Electric furnaces cost less to install upfront but run on electric resistance coils that can raise utility bills significantly. If your home doesn't have a gas line or you're interested in an all-electric option, a heat pump is the better electric alternative — see our [heat pump systems page](/heat-pump-systems) for how they compare. For most IE homeowners with existing gas service, a gas furnace remains the most cost-effective heating system.

---

**Card 4**
**Title:** Heat Pump as an Alternative
**Description:** If you're replacing an aging furnace and considering a full system upgrade, a heat pump delivers both heating and cooling in a single unit — eliminating the need for a separate AC system. Heat pumps work efficiently in the Inland Empire's mild winters and are eligible for federal tax credits under the Inflation Reduction Act (up to $2,000 for qualified heat pump installations) plus California utility rebates through programs like TECH Clean California. We size and install heat pumps for IE homes — see our [heat pump systems page](/heat-pump-systems) for details on whether it makes sense for your situation.

---

**Card 5**
**Title:** Ductwork Condition
**Description:** A new furnace pushing air through leaky or undersized ductwork wastes efficiency from day one. During the in-home estimate, we inspect the existing duct system and note any leaks, disconnects, or undersized runs that would limit the new system's performance. Duct sealing or modification is sometimes needed, and we'll include that scope in the quote so there are no surprises at install day.

---

**Card 6**
**Title:** Permits and Code Compliance
**Description:** Furnace installations in California require a building permit and inspection. We pull the permit, schedule the inspection, and handle the paperwork. Skipping the permit process creates liability if you sell the home — unpermitted HVAC work can block a real estate transaction. All our installations comply with California Title 24 energy efficiency requirements and current California Mechanical Code.

## Cost & Financing

**H2:** How Much Does Furnace Installation Cost?

**Furnace installation in the Inland Empire costs between $4,000 and $8,500 installed, with the range driven primarily by the system's AFUE efficiency rating, BTU capacity, and brand. A standard 90% AFUE two-stage system for a typical IE home falls in the $4,000–$5,500 range; a 95%+ variable-speed high-efficiency system runs $5,500–$8,500.**

Here's how the installed cost breaks down:

- **Standard efficiency (80–89% AFUE), single-stage:** $3,500–$5,000 installed
- **High efficiency (90–94% AFUE), two-stage:** $4,500–$6,500 installed
- **Premium efficiency (95%+ AFUE), variable-speed:** $6,000–$8,500 installed
- **Ductwork repairs or modifications (if needed):** $500–$2,000 additional
- **Permit fees:** $150–$400 (varies by city/county)

Cost also varies by system BTU size and installation complexity. We give you an exact number before any work begins.

**Is it worth upgrading to a high-efficiency furnace?**

**Upgrading from an 80% AFUE furnace to a 95%+ unit makes financial sense when you plan to stay in the home more than five years — ENERGY STAR data shows the annual heating bill savings typically offset the cost premium within four to seven years, even in the Inland Empire's shorter heating season.**

The Inland Empire's mild winters mean most households run their furnaces fewer hours per year than colder climates — which means a shorter heating season, but also a furnace that ages slower. Most replacements in the IE are driven by a system that's hit the 15–20 year mark and either broke down or became noticeably less efficient. When replacing at that age, moving to a 90%+ AFUE system is almost always the right call. We'll show you the math on your specific situation at the in-home estimate.

**Financing:** We offer financing options for furnace installations. Ask about current terms when you call — there's no reason to delay a system replacement because of upfront cost.

## FAQ

**H2:** Frequently Asked Questions About Furnace Installation

---

**Q1: How much does furnace installation cost in the Inland Empire?**
Furnace installation in the Inland Empire typically costs $4,000–$8,500 fully installed, depending on the system's efficiency rating (AFUE), BTU size, and whether ductwork modifications are needed. A standard 90% AFUE system for a typical IE home runs $4,500–$6,500. A premium 95%+ variable-speed system runs $6,000–$8,500. Permit fees add $150–$400 depending on your city. We provide a written quote after a free in-home estimate so you know the exact number before committing.

---

**Q2: What size furnace do I need?**
Furnace size is measured in BTUs per hour and is determined by a Manual J load calculation — not by square footage alone. Insulation quality, ceiling height, window count, and your local climate all factor in. An undersized furnace runs constantly; an oversized one cycles rapidly and wears out faster. We perform the Manual J calculation at no cost during the in-home estimate so your new system is sized correctly from day one.

---

**Q3: What is AFUE and why does it matter?**
AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency) measures what percentage of the fuel your furnace converts to heat. An 80% AFUE furnace wastes 20% of the gas it burns. A 95% AFUE furnace wastes only 5%. ENERGY STAR data shows that upgrading from an older 80% unit to a 90%+ system saves roughly 10–20% annually on heating bills. In the Inland Empire's milder climate, the payback period is longer than in colder states — but it still typically pays off within four to seven years, and the new system will run more quietly and consistently.

---

**Q4: Should I replace my furnace or repair it?**
Replace your furnace when the repair cost exceeds 50% of a new system's price and your unit is 15 or more years old. At that age, a costly repair often buys only a year or two of additional service, and modern systems are significantly more efficient. If your furnace is under 15 and the repair is minor, repair almost always wins financially. We give you an honest repair-vs-replace assessment on every service call — we don't push replacement to drive a larger ticket.

---

**Q5: How long does furnace installation take?**
Most residential furnace installations are complete in a single day — typically four to eight hours for a direct replacement. Installations requiring ductwork modifications or new gas line extensions take longer, sometimes two days. We give you a realistic timeline at the estimate appointment so you can plan accordingly.

---

**Q6: Do you pull permits for furnace installation?**
Yes. California requires a building permit for furnace installations, and we handle the permit application, coordination with your local building department, and the final inspection. All installations comply with California Title 24 energy efficiency requirements and current California Mechanical Code. Unpermitted installations create liability issues if you sell the home — we don't cut that corner.

---

**Q7: What brands of furnaces do you install?**
We install Carrier, Trane, Lennox, Rheem, York, Goodman, and Amana furnaces, among others. We'll recommend the brand and efficiency tier that best matches your home size, budget, and long-term efficiency goals. If you already have a brand preference, tell us — we'll source it.

---

**Q8: Is a heat pump a better option than a new furnace?**
It depends on your situation. A heat pump provides both heating and cooling in one system, making it a strong option if you're also replacing your AC at the same time. Heat pumps are eligible for federal tax credits up to $2,000 under the Inflation Reduction Act plus California utility rebates through programs like TECH Clean California. However, if your existing AC is newer and you only need to replace the furnace, a standalone high-efficiency furnace is usually more cost-effective. We'll walk through both options at the in-home estimate.
