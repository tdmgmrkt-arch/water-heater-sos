## Common Furnace Problems

**H2:** Common Furnace Problems We Fix

**Section intro (1 sentence):**
Inland Empire winters are mild by national standards, but a furnace failure during a cold snap in November or January is still a genuine emergency — here are the six problems our techs see most often.

---

**Card 1**
**Title:** Ignitor or Pilot Light Failure
**Description:** A failed ignitor is the single most common furnace repair call. Modern furnaces use a hot-surface ignitor (a fragile ceramic element) that glows red and lights the burner. When it cracks or burns out, the furnace won't fire at all — you'll hear the inducer fan run but no heat follows. Ignitor replacement typically costs $150–$400 and is usually a same-day fix since we carry common ignitors for Carrier, Lennox, Trane, and Rheem systems on the truck.

---

**Card 2**
**Title:** Dirty or Faulty Flame Sensor
**Description:** The flame sensor is a small metal rod that confirms the burner actually lit. When it gets coated with oxidation or residue, it tells the control board the flame isn't there — and the furnace shuts off after a few seconds. The symptom: your furnace starts, blows warm air briefly, then cuts out and tries again. A dirty flame sensor usually just needs cleaning; a failed sensor needs replacement. Either way, it's a fast repair when caught early.

---

**Card 3**
**Title:** Blower Motor Failure
**Description:** The blower motor pushes heated air through your ductwork and into the home. When it fails, the furnace may heat the heat exchanger but the warm air never reaches your rooms — or you may hear a loud grinding or squealing from the unit. Blower motor replacement runs $400–$800 depending on the motor type and your furnace model. Catching early signs (unusual noise, reduced airflow) prevents a complete shutdown during the coldest part of the season.

---

**Card 4**
**Title:** Cracked Heat Exchanger
**Description:** A cracked heat exchanger is the most serious furnace problem and a genuine safety hazard. The heat exchanger is the metal barrier between combustion gases (including carbon monoxide) and the air that circulates through your home. A crack allows CO to leak into your living space. Symptoms include a yellow or flickering burner flame, soot near the furnace, and CO detector alarms. If we find a cracked heat exchanger, we'll give you an honest assessment of whether repair or full replacement is the safer call — this is not a repair to defer.

---

**Card 5**
**Title:** Thermostat or Control Board Issue
**Description:** Sometimes the furnace itself is fine but the signal to run it isn't getting through. A misconfigured thermostat, a dead battery, or a failed control board can all mimic a furnace failure. Before calling, verify your thermostat is set to Heat, the temperature is set above the current room temp, and the fan is set to Auto (not On). If all that checks out and the furnace still won't start, the control board or a wiring fault is likely the culprit — and that's what our diagnostic covers.

---

**Card 6**
**Title:** Gas Valve or Pressure Problem
**Description:** The gas valve controls fuel delivery to the burners. When it fails or a gas pressure issue develops upstream, the furnace can't sustain combustion — or can't ignite at all. If you smell gas near the furnace, leave the house and call your gas utility first, then call us. We hold a C-20 HVAC contractor license and coordinate gas-line diagnostics alongside our repair work. Suspected gas issues also link to our [gas leak repair service](/gas-leak-repairs) for line-level problems.

## Cost & Repair-vs-Replace

**H2:** How Much Does Furnace Repair Cost?

**Furnace repair in the Inland Empire costs between $150 and $1,200, with most service calls falling in the $150–$600 range. A diagnostic call starts at $75–$150 (typically applied toward the repair), while a heat exchanger repair or replacement sits at the top end of the range at $500–$1,500.**

Here's how that repair range breaks down by component:

- **Diagnostic fee:** $75–$150 (applied toward the repair if you proceed)
- **Ignitor replacement:** $150–$400
- **Flame sensor cleaning or replacement:** $100–$250
- **Thermostat replacement:** $150–$350
- **Blower motor replacement:** $400–$800
- **Gas valve replacement:** $300–$600
- **Heat exchanger repair or replacement:** $500–$1,500

Cost varies by furnace age, brand, and parts availability. We give you an exact number before any work begins.

**Repair vs. Replace:**

**Replace your furnace when the repair cost exceeds 50% of a new system's price AND your unit is 15 or more years old; otherwise, repair is typically the better investment. According to ENERGY STAR, the average gas furnace has a useful lifespan of 15–20 years — which is why the age threshold matters as much as the cost number.**

When a repair quote feels high, the question is whether it's smarter to replace the system. A furnace at the 15-year mark that needs a $600 blower motor may still have 5–7 years of service life — repair often wins. But a furnace at 20 years with a cracked heat exchanger is almost always a replacement candidate, both for safety and for the efficiency gains a modern 90%+ AFUE system delivers over an aging 80% unit. We'll tell you honestly where your system stands. If replacement makes more sense, we'll walk you through [furnace installation options](/furnace-installation) and can schedule that same week. If the repair is solid, we'll do the repair.

## FAQ

**H2:** Frequently Asked Questions About Furnace Repair

---

**Q1: How much does furnace repair cost?**
Most furnace repairs in the Inland Empire range from $150 to $600, depending on the problem. An ignitor replacement runs $150–$400. Blower motor replacement costs $400–$800. A heat exchanger repair or replacement is the most expensive at $500–$1,500. We provide a firm, upfront quote before starting any work so you know exactly what you're paying.

---

**Q2: Should I repair or replace my furnace?**
Repair your furnace when the repair cost is less than 50% of a new system and your unit is under 15 years old; replace it when the repair exceeds that threshold or the system is older. ENERGY STAR puts the average gas furnace lifespan at 15–20 years. If your furnace is under 15 and the repair is minor, fixing it almost always makes financial sense. If it's 20 years old with a cracked heat exchanger, replacement is the safer and more cost-effective path.

---

**Q3: Why is my furnace blowing cold air?**
A furnace blows cold air for one of four main reasons: a failed ignitor or pilot light, a dirty flame sensor that shuts the burner off, an overheated system that tripped the limit switch, or a thermostat set to "Fan On" instead of "Auto." A quick check: confirm the thermostat is set to Heat with the temperature above the current room reading and the fan set to Auto. If the furnace still blows cold after that, call us — it's usually a same-day diagnosis and fix.

---

**Q4: Is a cracked heat exchanger dangerous?**
Yes. A cracked heat exchanger is the most serious furnace problem because it can allow carbon monoxide — a colorless, odorless gas — to enter your home's air supply. Signs include a yellow or flickering burner flame, soot deposits near the furnace, and CO detector alarms. If you suspect a cracked heat exchanger, shut the furnace off and call us immediately. We inspect the heat exchanger on every service call as a standard safety check.

---

Yes — we provide 24/7 emergency furnace repair throughout the Inland Empire.
